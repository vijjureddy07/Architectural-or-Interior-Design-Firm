The Case for Biophilic Design:
Why Nature Belongs in Every Room

Isabel Voss

Principal Designer

· March 18, 2026 · 8 min read

When we integrate natural elements into architectural and interior space — living walls, raw stone, diffused daylight, water features — we tap into something ancient in human psychology. The research confirms what intuition long suspected: proximity to nature reduces cortisol levels, sharpens cognitive focus, and elevates mood in measurable ways.

The term "biophilia" was popularised by biologist E.O. Wilson in his 1984 book of the same name. It describes our innate tendency to seek connections with other living systems. But for designers, biophilic design has evolved far beyond placing a few potted plants near a window.

"A truly biophilic space doesn't just reference nature — it extends nature. The boundary between inside and outside dissolves."

The Three Dimensions of Biophilic Design

At Studio Noir, we approach biophilic design across three interconnected dimensions: direct nature contact, indirect nature representation, and spatial conditions that evoke natural environments.

Direct contact means literal integration — living moss walls, interior water features, plants that have genuine presence in the spatial composition. Indirect representation encompasses natural materials like stone, timber, and leather; forms that echo organic shapes; and the use of texture that rewards close inspection the way bark or stone does.

Spatial Conditions and the Psychology of Refuge

The third dimension — spatial conditions — is perhaps the most nuanced. Evolutionary psychology suggests we're drawn to spaces that offer both prospect (a wide view, a sense of openness and opportunity) and refuge (a sheltered position, enclosure, safety). These competing needs explain why the most successful living spaces often pair open-plan zones with intimate alcoves.

Ceiling height is a powerful lever here. We use double-height volumes for communal and social areas, dropping to more intimate proportions for reading nooks and sleeping spaces. The shift isn't just aesthetic — it's neurological.

Material Specificity

Materials are where biophilic intention becomes tangible. There's a meaningful difference between engineered stone and bookmatched marble — the former is uniform and industrial; the latter is a geological artefact, unique and unrepeatable. Similarly, solid hardwood flooring ages and develops character; engineered alternatives, however practical, don't carry the same emotional resonance.

This doesn't mean biophilic interiors require extravagant budgets. Textured linen, raw plaster walls, hand-thrown ceramics, and unfinished brass hardware all communicate naturalistic warmth at accessible price points. What matters is intentionality — selecting materials that reward engagement rather than deterring it.

The Role of Light

Possibly the most powerful biophilic tool available to architects and interior designers is daylight. We specify windows generously, position glazing to capture morning light in bedrooms and eastern-facing living areas, and design shading that modulates glare without sacrificing connection to the outside world.

Artificial lighting is designed to complement natural rhythms. Warm, lower colour temperatures (2700K–3000K) in the evening; brighter, cooler task lighting during working hours. Dimming control throughout so the occupant can tune their environment to their activity and time of day.
